Original · ancient Greek

Ὅ τί ποτε τοῦτό εἰμι, σαρκία ἐστὶ καὶ πνευμάτιον καὶ τὸ ἡγεμονικόν. ἄφες τὰ βιβλία· μηκέτι σπῶ. οὐ δέδοται, ἀλλ’ ὡς ἤδη ἀποθνῄσκων τῶν μὲν σαρκίων καταφρόνησον· λύθρος καὶ ὀστάρια καὶ κροκύφαντος, ἐκ νεύρων, φλεβίων, ἀρτηριῶν πλεγμάτιον. θέασαι δὲ καὶ τὸ πνεῦμα ὁποῖόν τί ἐστιν· ἄνεμος, οὐδὲ ἀεὶ τὸ αὐτό, ἀλλὰ πάσης ὥρας ἐξεμούμενον καὶ πάλιν ῥοφούμενον. τρίτον οὖν ἐστι τὸ ἡγεμονικόν. ὧδε ἐπινοήθητι· γέρων εἶ· μηκέτι τοῦτο ἐάσῃς δουλεῦσαι, μηκέτι καθ’ ὁρμὴν ἀκοινώνητον νευροσπαστηθῆναι, μηκέτι τὸ εἱμαρμένον ἢ παρὸν δυσχερᾶναι ἢ μέλλον ὑπιδέσθαι.

Leopold · Teubner 1908
Commentary

Genre and place in the book. The second passage of Book II, coming immediately after the programmatic morning meditation. If 02-01 prepares one for the meeting with others, 02-02 prepares one for the meeting with oneself: what am I, once social roles and habitual self-identifications are stripped away. The passage is the master-key of the whole corpus on Hadot's reading: its closing three imperatives cover all three disciplines (assent, action, desire) — which is why it became the basis of La Citadelle intérieure.

Structure of the argument. It unfolds in two movements — analytical and normative.

  1. Analytical. "Whatever this thing is that I am, it is TERMa scrap of flesh, TERMa little breath, and the TERMruling part." A threefold division marked by deprecatory morphological diminutives (σαρκία, πνευμάτιον, then ὀστάρια, πλεγμάτιον). Each part is then stripped to its material:
  • σαρκία → λύθρος καὶ ὀστάρια καὶ κροκύφαντος (the exercise of EXERCISEphysical definition, the image MOTIFflesh-as-network);
  • πνευμάτιον → ἄνεμος ἐξεμούμενον καὶ ῥοφούμενον ("a breath, exhaled and inhaled again");
  • what remains is τὸ ἡγεμονικόν — the only thing to which the "I" can be applied (see the doctrine DOGMAself-is-hegemonikon).
  1. Normative. "You are an old man" (γέρων εἶ — the posture of EXERCISEthe memory of death). Three μηκέτι ("no longer"):
  • μηκέτι δουλεῦσαι — let the ruling part no longer be enslaved (the general posture of DOGMAthe dichotomy);
  • μηκέτι καθ' TERMὁρμὴν ἀκοινώνητον MOTIFνευροσπαστηθῆναι — the discipline of action: do not let an unsocial impulse twitch you like a puppet on strings;
  • μηκέτι τὸ TERMεἱμαρμένον … δυσχερᾶναι … ὑπιδέσθαι — the discipline of desire: be not vexed at your present lot, nor fear what is to come (DOGMAamor-fati).

All three disciplines in a single passage. This is the reason Hadot chose precisely 02-02 as the "map" of Marcus's philosophy. In one paragraph:

  • assent: correct self-identification (I am only the TERMruling part);
  • action: the social quality of the TERMimpulse;
  • desire: acceptance of TERMfate.

Stylistics. Deprecatory morphology: σαρκία, πνευμάτιον, ὀστάρια, φλέβια, πλεγμάτιον — five diminutives in a single sentence. The method is not rhetorical but analytical: the morphology itself draws the line between "I" and "not-I." In parallel runs the verbal chain νεῦρα → νευρόσπαστον: the material of the body (sinews, veins) and the material of the puppet (the strings on which it is twitched) are the same word; the only question is who is pulling whom (see the images MOTIFflesh-as-network and MOTIFpuppet).

A terminological subtlety. In Stoic orthodoxy the human being is bipartite: body and pneuma (where rational pneuma ≡ ἡγεμονικόν). Marcus's triad "flesh / breath / ruling part" is not a doctrinal division but a methodological move: to isolate the dense matter (σάρξ), the subtle matter (πνεῦμα in the narrow sense of "breath"), and the rational element proper (ἡγεμονικόν). The triad recurs at Med. 3.16 and 12.3 — it is to be read as a meditative device, not as an ontological scheme.

Parallels. The programmatic formula "what belongs to me" — Epictetus Disc. 1.1 and Ench. 1; the famous «ψυχάριον εἶ βαστάζον νεκρόν» ("you are a little soul carrying a corpse"), which Marcus ascribes to Epictetus at Med. 4.41. The posture "as one already dying" — Sen. Ep. 26 ("the daily preparation for death"), Ep. 76, Ep. 101; Plat. Phd. 64a (philosophy as μελέτη θανάτου, the practice of dying). The image νευρόσπαστον — Plat. Leg. 644d–645a (but positively in Plato: god pulls the "golden string"); in Marcus, the inversion.
